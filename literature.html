The Reflection
In the dim light of a forgotten room, the book lies open, its glossy cover a dark pool, reflecting the intruder it denounces. Anti-Tech Revolution: Why and How— words etched in pale script, a manifesto against the machine, yet there, upon its face, gleams the cold eye of the smartphone, capturing the very scene it abhors. The prophet's tome, born of isolation and fury, warns of the inexorable advance, the self-propagating system that devours freedom, turns man into surrogate activity, binds the wild spirit to circuits and screens. But behold the mirror: the device hovers, obedient servant, framing the indictment in its own image, immortalizing the warning through the poison it names. How profound the betrayal— the revolution photographed by its enemy, the howl against progress echoed in pixels, the dream of collapse preserved in the cloud. In this accidental tableau, the irony uncoils like smoke from a ruined forest: even rebellion cannot escape the web, and the voice crying wilderness is amplified by the very chains it seeks to shatter. We gaze into the reflection, and see only ourselves, trapped in the glass, forever documenting our own enslavement.
